Two Deceptions, One Goal: A Curated History

1. The Survivor's Tale

Survivorship bias is the "silent evidence" problem. We only see the funds that survived. The thousands that failed are liquidated or merged, erasing their poor performance from the public record and creating a deceptively optimistic view of history.

Source: Vanguard, "Putting a value on your value," 2019.

2. The AUM-Weighted Deception

A manager can have a "winning" track record in percentage terms but still be a net loser of investor capital. This happens when they post huge losses on a large asset base after attracting investors with wins on a small one.

Where the Bias Hides

The severity of survivorship bias varies. Opaque markets with voluntary reporting, like hedge funds, are the most distorted.

Source: Aggregated academic studies (e.g., Elton, Gruber & Blake, 1996)

Conclusion: Think Like a Skeptic

Reported performance is a curated narrative. The twin risks of survivorship bias and AUM-weighted deception mean investors must look beyond the advertised returns. A manager's true value is revealed not by their best track record, but by the completeness and transparency of their entire history.
